How TaloNet actually works

A counter-UAS mothership that nets a hostile drone in flight and recovers it intact, then strips its memory to the launch point. This page explains the system end to end.

1 — Capture over kill

Every other answer to a hostile drone ends in a fireball or a jammed band, and both throw away the most valuable thing in the sky: the drone itself. Shooting it down scatters debris over whatever is below and destroys the evidence of who sent it. Jamming takes down your own datalinks along with the target's.

TaloNet takes the opposite approach. A larger mothership drone patrols the airspace; an operator identifies a hostile UAS and commits the intercept; a launcher places a net over it. What happens next depends on the target:

  • A reconnaissance drone is taken alive. The capture net is cinched shut and the catch is flown home intact — a reusable airframe and a complete, unaltered record of where it launched and what it saw, ready for exploitation.
  • A kamikaze drone is neutralised at a distance. A second, larger stand-off net is fired from outside the blast radius to foul its rotors; its tether is then cut and the whole catch is dropped over open ground. The interceptor never closes to contact with a live warhead.

One drone carries enough information to compromise the operation that launched it. TaloNet's job is to bring it back whole and read it.

An onboard machine-vision autonomy loop was considered and deliberately dropped: a human reacts faster than a multi-second inference, so the interceptor is flown manually rather than handing the trigger to a model.

2 — The interceptor (“Peregrine”)

The mothership is a coaxial X8 octocopter — four arms, each carrying two motors stacked top and bottom — sized to carry a net launcher, a captured drone, and a full sensor suite while still keeping enough thrust in reserve to chase a fast target and snatch it out of the air. The coaxial layout also means it can stay airborne and controllable if a single motor fails.

3 — The engagement

Onboard electro-optical and infrared sensors hold the track and feed a stabilized picture to the operator, who classifies the contact and commits the intercept. The interceptor carries two nets on one aimed turret, and the operator picks which one to fire based on the target.

CRADLE — the capture net (reconnaissance). The small self-cinching net is fired over the target, a drawstring purses its mouth shut so the catch cannot work free, and a winch raises it against the belly. The interceptor carries it home in the net itself — there is no separate cage — for recovery and exploitation.

TRAWLER — the stand-off net (kamikaze). For a one-way attack drone the interceptor never closes to contact. A larger, denser net is fired from outside the blast radius to foul the target's rotors and stop it falling on anything that matters; once clear ground is confirmed, a cord-cutter severs the tether and the whole catch is jettisoned. No cinch, no winch, no approach.

Every armed action — firing either net, cinching, cord-cutting, releasing — is locked behind an explicit armed state, and an emergency stop latches the whole payload so nothing can fire while it is engaged.

4 — The operator cockpit

The interceptor is flown from a ground-control application that runs on a laptop. A realistic EO/IR first-person view fills the screen beneath a military heads-up display: a boresight and pitch ladder, a bank arc, heading, speed and altitude tapes, a box framing the hostile UAS, and a green reticle showing exactly where the net is currently aimed. The same operator flies the aircraft and aims and fires the net, all from the keyboard.

The flight controls cover pitch, roll, yaw and throttle; a second cluster slews the net aim in pan and tilt; a mode key switches between the two nets; and dedicated keys fire the selected net, cinch it shut, cut the stand-off tether, release the catch, toggle the armed state, trigger an emergency stop, reset, and command return-to-home. The reticle changes colour and size with the selected net, and the wrong-mode key is refused — you cannot cinch a stand-off net or cut a recovery net's tether.

The cockpit is not a mock-up. It speaks the same MAVLink command and telemetry protocol used by real flight controllers, so the exact same software drives a desktop vehicle emulator, an ArduPilot software-in-the-loop simulation, or a real Pixhawk flight controller over a serial link — only the connection target changes. Net aim is sent as real servo commands; arming, firing both nets, cutting the tether and return-to-home are real flight-controller commands; and the heads-up display is driven by live attitude, speed, GPS and battery telemetry coming back from the vehicle.

5 — The nets & how they are aimed

Neither net is simply dropped — both are launched from one shared pan/tilt gimbal so the operator can place them on a target that is off to the side or above. The turret swings to the aimed direction and casts the selected net, which spreads as it flies. The small CRADLE net's mouth is then drawn shut by a drawstring spool — a separate mechanism from the winch that raises and lowers the catch — while the large TRAWLER net is never recovered: a cord-cutter on its tether severs it so the catch falls away.

The aim and payload channels are a single shared definition, so the gimbal range in the physical model, the travel the operator can command in the cockpit, and the wiring on the flight controller all refer to the same numbers. Changing a limit in one place changes all three.

6 — Defending the interceptor

The interceptor's own navigation and command links are the obvious way to take it down mid-catch, so it hardens itself against exactly that. Each layer implements a published, verified algorithm or relies on an audited open-source implementation rather than home-grown cryptography, and the detection logic is light enough to run on the flight computer itself — it detects attacks aimed at the interceptor and refuses to act on bad data, so the catch finishes.

  • Authenticated satellite navigation. Galileo's navigation-message authentication (a one-way key chain with a delayed message authentication code) lets the receiver verify that the GNSS signals it is using are genuine. This is the only layer that can catch a spoof that is otherwise perfectly self-consistent.
  • Integrity monitoring. Receiver-autonomous integrity monitoring checks the satellite measurements against each other and statistically detects and excludes a faulty or hostile satellite before it can corrupt the position fix.
  • Consistency checks. A set of sensor cross-checks catch spoofing and jamming that has not yet been authenticated away — sudden position jumps, navigation-filter disagreement, abnormal signal strength, clock jumps, and disagreement between satellite constellations.
  • Authenticated command link. Every command carries a cryptographic signature and a monotonic timestamp, so forged or replayed commands are rejected. The link layer adds a sliding-window anti-replay check and watches for jamming or takeover.

These layers feed a single monitor that produces one plain-language verdict every cycle, and the flight stack acts on it. The logic is deliberately fail-safe toward inertial navigation — it never silently trusts a suspect fix:

7 — Reading the captured drone

Once a hostile drone has been captured and secured, its storage is read to answer the question that decides the next move: where did this come from? This is exploitation of a device you already hold — the output is targeting-grade intelligence for a commander: the launch point, the base, the next target on the route.

The work runs as a fixed, ordered procedure with the original evidence treated as untouchable:

StepWhat happens
1 — IntakeThe item is logged into a tamper-evident custody record.
2 — ImagingA read-only, write-blocked copy of the storage is taken; the original is never written to.
3 — Integrity checkThe copy's cryptographic hash must match the original. If it does not, the run stops and no report is produced.
4 — ContentThe image is inventoried and searched for flight logs and other relevant files.
5 — Flight logsEach recovered log is parsed by a verified open-source parser for that format.
6 — TrajectoryThe track is reconstructed and the launch point and likely target are estimated, with a map.
7 — ReportA threat-intelligence report is assembled together with the full custody record.

The parsers are not guesswork. Each flight-log format — ArduPilot, PX4, NMEA, DJI — is read by an established, audited open-source tool, and the storage is imaged with standard forensic imaging tooling. From the reconstructed track the system pulls far more than a single coordinate: the hostile launch point with an uncertainty radius, the operating-radius range ring that hints at the enemy base, repeated departure points across multiple sorties, the planned waypoints (which point at the next intended target), firmware and board attribution, geofence and radio parameters, and geotagged reconnaissance imagery — all on a timeline.

Every estimate carries a confidence and the evidence it rests on; nothing is asserted as certainty, and the launch point is meant to be cross-checked by independent means. The custody log is append-only and hash-chained, so any tampering is evident and a commander can trust the provenance of what they are reading.

In the field this is packaged as a sealed appliance, ForensIQ-1: insert the captured drone's memory card into a write-block slot and the whole procedure runs end to end, printing a threat-intelligence report on a built-in thermal printer and archiving the full report to an evidence drive.

8 — Engineering & validation

The airframe and the forensic appliance are both modeled parametrically, so dimensions are driven by a small set of named parameters rather than hand-drawn — change the wheelbase, the propeller size, or the net dimensions and the model rebuilds itself, and the net-aim angles in the model are the same ones the cockpit and wiring use. Both models pass a watertight (manifold) geometry check, so they export cleanly for manufacturing or further CAD work.

The software is covered by an automated test suite that runs in continuous integration on every change: it is linted and then exercised on Python 3.12. The tests cover the navigation-defense layers (authentication, integrity monitoring, spoof and jamming detection, command signing and the fused verdict), the cockpit's control core and command link, and the forensic core — the custody chain, the trajectory math, and the log parsers. The core logic is written to run without any third-party packages, so most of the suite runs with nothing installed.
